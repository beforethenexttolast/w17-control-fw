Electrical — what connects to where
ELEC-01

System overview

elrs-joystick-control"]
    PC -->|USB| FT["USB-UART FT232"]

Two completely separate radio links. The control link (2.4 GHz ELRS) and the video link (5.8 GHz WiFi) share nothing but the battery — that's why TX power never affects video range. Everything below is a zoom-in on one box of this picture.

ELEC-02

Power distribution — the two rails

flowchart TD
  BAT["2S LiPo 7.4V
XT60"] --> Y["XT60 Y-split"]
  Y --> ESC["ESC"]
  Y --> B1["BEC #1
5V 5A — clean rail"]
  Y --> B2["BEC #2
5-6V 5A — servo rail"]
  ESC --> MOT["Brushless motor"]
  B1 --> CAM["Camera"]
  B1 --> WIFI["WiFi module VDD5.0"]
  B1 --> E1["ESP32 #1"]
  B1 --> E2["ESP32 #2"]
  B1 --> RX["ELRS RX"]
  B1 --> LED["WS2812 LEDs"]
  B2 --> SVS["Steering servo"]
  B2 --> PAN["Pan servo"]
  B2 --> TILT["Tilt servo"]
  B2 --> DRS["DRS servo"]

One battery, split at an XT60 Y into three: the ESC (motor), and two BECs. BEC #1 is the quiet rail for everything sensitive — camera, WiFi, both ESP32s, the RX, LEDs. BEC #2 feeds only the servos, so their current spikes and electrical noise never reach the video.

Critical: every GND must be tied together (battery, ESC, both BECs, ESP32s, camera, module). A shared ground is what makes the signals between boards actually work.

ELEC-03

Control signal chain (gamepad → car)

flowchart LR
  DS["DualShock"] -->|USB| PC["PC
maps buttons to channels"]
  PC -->|USB serial| FT["FT232
3.3V"]
  FT -->|CRSF TX to RX pin| TX["ELRS TX module
set 25-100 mW"]
  P5["5V"] -.-> TX
  TX ==>|2.4 GHz| RX["ELRS RX RP1"]
  TX16["TX16S
same bind phrase"] ==>|2.4 GHz| RX
  RX -->|CRSF over UART| E1["ESP32 #1"]

The PC turns your gamepad into CRSF (the same data a normal radio sends) and pushes it out the FT232's TX line into the TX module's signal pin; the module needs its own 5 V. The TX16S binds to the same RX with the same bind phrase, so it's a drop-in backup transmitter — only one transmits at a time.

ELEC-04

ESP32 #1 (control) — inputs & outputs

flowchart LR
  RX["ELRS RX"] -->|CRSF in| E1["ESP32 #1
control"]
  VD["Voltage divider
2 resistors"] -->|ADC pin| E1
  E1 -->|CH2 PWM| ESC["ESC throttle"]
  E1 -->|CH1 PWM| SVS["Steering servo"]
  E1 -->|CH3 PWM| PAN["Pan servo"]
  E1 -->|CH4 PWM| TILT["Tilt servo"]
  E1 -->|CH6 PWM| DRS["DRS servo"]
  E1 -->|UART| E2["ESP32 #2"]

This is the brain. It reads one CRSF stream in, then fans out a PWM (servo) signal to the ESC and each servo — one signal wire each, all sharing the rail's 5 V and a common ground. CH5 gear, CH7 boost, CH8 overtake have no wire of their own — they're handled in firmware (virtual gearbox / ERS) and passed to ESP32 #2.

Pin names are examples — the actual GPIO numbers live in your esp32_car_control.ino. What matters is the topology: one signal wire per actuator.

ELEC-05

ESP32 #2 (sound & light) — outputs

flowchart LR
  E1["ESP32 #1"] -->|UART| E2["ESP32 #2
sound + light"]
  E2 -->|I2S: BCLK LRC DIN| AMP["MAX98357A amp"]
  AMP -->|2 wires| SPK["Speaker 4 ohm"]
  E2 -->|data via 330 ohm| LED["WS2812 strip
brake + halo"]

The second ESP32 takes commands from the first over a 3-wire UART, then drives two things: the engine sound via I2S (three signal wires — BCLK, LRC, DIN — into the MAX98357A, which powers the speaker), and the addressable LEDs via a single data line through a ~330 Ω resistor. Brake-red and halo are just different pixels in code.

ELEC-06

Video — camera ↔ WiFi module

flowchart LR
  B1["BEC #1 5V"] -->|VDD5.0| WIFI["BL-M8812EU2"]
  CAM["OpenIPC camera
SSC338Q + IMX415"] -->|USB Dplus to DP| WIFI
  CAM -->|USB Dminus to DM| WIFI
  CAM ---|GND| WIFI
  WIFI --- ANT["Antennas J0 J1
connect BEFORE power"]
  WIFI --- HS["Heatsink
fit first"]
  FT["FT232 UART"] -->|recovery + flash| CAM
  WIFI ==>|5.8 GHz WiFi AP| LAP["Laptop"]

Four solder joints make the camera-to-module link: D+→DP, D−→DM, GND→GND, and 5 V→VDD5.0 from the clean BEC (not the camera's own USB rail). The same FT232 from the control side doubles as your camera console for flashing/recovery.

Two rules that protect the module: antennas on J0/J1 before any power, and the heatsink fitted before first power-on.

Part B
Mechanical — how the parts join up
MECH-01

Drivetrain — motor to rear wheels

flowchart LR
  MOT["Brushless motor
3.175 mm shaft"] -->|mesh 48P| PIN["Pinion 28T"]
  PIN -->|mesh 48P| SPUR["Spur 75T"]
  SPUR --> PUL1["Drive pulley"]
  PUL1 -->|toothed belt| PUL2["Rear-axle pulley"]
  PUL2 --> AXLE["Rear axle"]
  AXLE -->|rides in| BRG["2x bearings 12x21x5"]
  AXLE -->|spaced by| SPC["2x 14 mm spacers"]
  AXLE --> WHL["Rear wheels 12 mm hex"]

Power flows left to right. The pinion and spur must be the same 48-pitch or the teeth won't mesh — the one mechanical mistake that stops everything. The belt then carries drive to the rear axle, which spins in its bearings; the 14 mm metal spacers keep the printed holders off the hot axle.

MECH-02

Steering — servo to front wheels

flowchart LR
  SV["Steering servo"] --> HORN["25T horn"]
  HORN --> ROD["M4 40 mm rod"]
  ROD --> TR["M4 22 mm tie rods
+ 32 mm turnbuckles"]
  TR -->|clip onto| BS["M3 ball studs"]
  BS --> KN["Steering knuckles"]
  KN -->|pivot on| KP["King pins 30 mm"]
  KN --> FW["Front wheels 12 mm hex"]

The servo arm pushes a rod that splits to both front knuckles through the tie rods. The turnbuckles are the adjustable links — lengthening or shortening them sets your toe (and they're the bits that snap in a crash). The knuckles swing on the king pins.

Centre the servo in firmware before you attach the linkage, or the wheels won't sit straight at neutral.

MECH-03

One front corner — how it all relates

flowchart TD
  CH["Chassis"] --> AARM["A-arm"]
  AARM -->|pivots on| KP["King pin 30 mm"]
  CH --> SHK["Front shock 51 mm"]
  SHK --> AARM
  KP --> KN["Steering knuckle"]
  KN --> HUB["Front hub"]
  HUB -->|holds| BRG["2x bearings 8x12x3.5"]
  BRG --> AX["Stub axle"]
  AX --> WH["Front wheel"]
  TR["Tie rod"] --> KN

A single front wheel ties four jobs together: the A-arm handles up/down (damped by the shock), the king pin lets the knuckle steer, the two bearings let the wheel spin, and the tie rod connects it to the steering. Build each corner as a unit.

MECH-04

Rear axle assembly

flowchart TD
  HLD["Printed bearing holders"] -->|hold| BRG["2x bearings 12x21x5"]
  BRG --> AXLE["Rear axle"]
  SPC["2x 14 mm metal spacers"] --> AXLE
  PUL["Rear pulley"] --> AXLE
  SPUR["Spur 75T"] --> PUL
  AXLE --> WHL["Rear wheels"]
  SHK["Rear shock 68 mm — central"] --> HLD

The rear axle threads through bearings held in the chassis, with the belt pulley and spur riding on it. Unlike the front's two shocks, the rear uses one central 68 mm damper — the F1 layout.

MECH-05

Fasteners & inserts — what holds it together

flowchart LR
  PART["Printed part"] -->|melt in with iron| INS["Heat-set M3 insert"]
  BOLT["M3 bolt"] -->|screws into| INS
  BOLT -.->|metal to metal: thread-lock| NUT["M3 nut"]
  GUIDE["Printed guide-rod"] -->|replace with| SLV["D5xM3 metal sleeve"]

The trick that makes a printed car durable: a brass heat-set insert melts into the plastic so a steel M3 bolt bites metal threads, not plastic. Use blue thread-lock only where metal meets metal (never into plastic). The D5×M3 sleeves replace wear-prone printed guide rods up front.
